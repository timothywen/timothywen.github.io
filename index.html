</h1>

Throughout the history of cinema, as film making software and technology developed, the quality of movies have improved by leaps and bounds. From animations to documentaries, we have seen improvements in a film's sound quality, video quality, production, and even movie length. This is especially true when we take a look at movies created by major studios. For example, if we look back at Pixar's first animation, Toy Story, released in 1995, we can see that it only had a length of 81 minutes. Compartively, all their movies in recents years have consistently been 100 minutes each. Although that may not seem like much, those extra 19 minutes constitute a 23.5% increase in movie duration. That extra 19 minutes means an extra 19 minutes of additional animation, voice acting, editing, and more.

Now this begs the question, did this increase of 23.5% improve ratings, and if it did, by how much? As an avid movie watcher who has benefited from many hours of improved video quality, my goal of this tutorial is to represent the relationships various factors have on a movie's ratings. For this assignment, I will only be analyzing movies and not short films or TV series.

Data Collection/Curation

To begin, we first need to find a comprehensive dataset that contains information on the year a movie came out, its runtime, and its rating. Thankfully, IMDb, the online database of information on all kinds of video productions, provides several databases with various information using a movie's unique alphanumeric identifier. To access these databases, you could visit https://www.imdb.com/interfaces/.

Because datasets are saved differently, we will need to convert IMDb's dataset that is stored as a tsv, a tab separated values, into a csv, a comma separated value. This way, we can use the pandas command, 'read_csv' to turn this table into a dataframe. A dataframe is a datastructure that stores rows of values that have characteristics that are stored within the columns. (More information about the functions of a dataframe and how it can be used can be found at https://pandas.pydata.org/docs/reference/api/pandas.DataFrame.html)

Next, because different IMDb datasets store different information, we will have to merge two of the databases into one. When doing this, it is important to not merge the two datasets incorrectly. In this case, IMDb gives a unique classifier to each title under the column 'tconst.' We can use this consistency to combine the tables into one.

def moviesPerYear(df,x,xlab,ylab, title):
  matplotlib.rcParams['figure.figsize'] = [13,10]
  fig,ax = plt.subplots()

  #sorts the years to be unique
  years = info[x].unique()
  years.sort()

  #counts up the number of movies per year
  movies = []
  for year in years:
    curYear = info[info[x] == year]
    movies.append(len(curYear))

  #clumps years into decades
  dec = []
  mov_dec = []
  sum = 0
  index = 0
  start = years[0]
  last_year = 0
  
  #loops through and every unique year correlates to new decade
  for year in years:
    if year % 10 == 0:
      mov_dec.append(sum)
      sum = 0
      dec.append(str(start) + '-' + str(year - 1))
      start = year

    sum = movies[index]
    index = index + 1
    last_year = year

  #adds the incomplete decade 
  dec.append(str(start)+"-"+str(last_year))
  mov_dec.append(sum)

  plt.bar(dec, mov_dec, align='center',alpha = .6)
  plt.ylabel('Number of Movies')
  plt.xlabel('Decades')
  plt.title('Number of Movies Released Per Decade')
